## Feb 15 — Travel to Marrakech (10:30pm)
Population: ~1.1M. The "Red City" — named for its ochre sandstone walls built in 1122. Fun fact: the word "Morocco" comes from "Marrakech."

### Airport → Earth Hostel
- 15-20 min taxi, 5km
- Petit taxi from rank outside arrivals (beige cars)
- Agree price BEFORE getting in — 70-100 MAD (€7-10). They will try 200-300.
- Say confidently: "المدينة، مية درهم" Walk to next taxi if they refuse.
- Have exact cash (they claim no change)
- Taxi stops at nearest medina gate — short walk to hostel
- Alternative: ask hostel about pickup (+212 6510-542-81)

### 🏛 History & Culture
- The country's name comes from this city — in Persian, Urdu, and South Asian languages Morocco is still called "Marrakech"
- 19km of fortification walls with 200+ defensive towers, built 1122-1123. The small holes across the walls were left from scaffolding and let the walls "breathe"
- Koutoubia Mosque's golden spheres — legend says they're melted-down jewelry of Sultana Fatima, donated as penance for breaking her Ramadan fast

### 👁 What to Notice
- Red sandstone/mud everywhere — the ochre palette that gives it the "Red City" name
- Zellige tilework — hand-cut geometric patterns in blue (sky/water), green (paradise), white (purity). No people or animals per Islamic tradition
- Riad architecture — modest wooden doors hiding magnificent courtyards. Look for the دويرية / douriya (bent entrance) that blocks the view inside when doors open
- Tadelakt plaster — waterproof lime polished with river stones and black soap, silky marble-like finish
- Mashrabiya screens — carved wooden latticework on windows, see out without being seen

### 👤 People
- Djellabas (long hooded robes) everywhere — daily wear, not just special occasions
- Women mix Western clothing with djellaba/hijab. Men wear button-downs, polos, chinos mixed with traditional pieces
- Colorful scarves, patterned shawls, handcrafted leather babouche slippers
- Kaftan for special occasions — embellished floor-length robes with wide sleeves

## Feb 19 — Essaouira: Medina & Coast
Population: ~77K. Vibe: Laid-back coastal, artistic, Gnawa music, windy.

### 🏛 History & Culture
- Game of Thrones filmed here — Essaouira became Astapor in Season 3 (where Daenerys got the Unsullied). HBO changed almost nothing, the ramparts were perfect as-is
- Portuguese built the original ramparts in the 1500s. Current walls from the 1760s in Vauban military architecture style
- UNESCO World Heritage fortified seaport — rare example of late 18th-century military architecture in North Africa
- Gnawa music capital — Sufi ritual music tradition from Sub-Saharan Africa, UNESCO Intangible Heritage since 2019. Annual Gnaoua Festival draws hundreds of thousands

### 👁 What to Notice
- White walls with blue accents — completely different palette from Marrakech's red. Portuguese/French colonial influence
- Vauban-style stone ramparts — European military architecture, weathered and massive, with old cannons still in place
- Less zellige tilework than Marrakech — simpler, more utilitarian, reflecting a working port not an imperial city
- Thick walls, small windows — built to handle Atlantic winds and salt air
- Woodworking artisans — thuya wood carved into intricate boxes, chess sets, furniture. Unique to this region

### 👤 People
- Gnawa musicians in sky-blue djellabas — embroidered tunics, babouche slippers, caps with cowrie shells and long tassels. The blue is distinctive to Gnawa tradition
- More bohemian/artist community than other Moroccan cities — attracted creatives for decades
- Coastal relaxed vibe — still conservative Morocco but noticeably more laid-back than Marrakech

### 🌞 Ramparts → UNESCO medina

UNESCO medina — blue doors, silver souks, woodworking artisans.

## Feb 24 — Travel to Sidi Ifni
Population: ~20K. Vibe: Art Deco time capsule, fishing town.

### 🏛 History & Culture
- Spanish colony until 1969 — originally founded by Canary Islanders in 1476, known as Santa Cruz de la Mar Pequeña
- The colonials fled suddenly in 1969 — boarded planes and abandoned the city, leaving behind a perfectly preserved 1930s Art Deco time capsule
- Most unique architecture in Morocco — sandwiched between the Sahara, Anti-Atlas mountains, and the Atlantic. Nothing else like it in the country
- Recently restored — buildings classified as monuments by the Moroccan Ministry of Culture

- Moorish Art Deco fusion — geometric patterns combined with Spanish colonial elements. Exists almost nowhere else on earth
- Whitewashed buildings with vivid blue trim — curved balconies, symmetrical façades, decorative ironwork

- Look for arched windows, ornate ironwork railings, and Art Deco geometric door patterns

## Berber Spirituality 🕯

Morocco is 99% Sunni Muslim (Maliki school — moderate, not Gulf-style). The king is "Commander of the Faithful." But underneath the official Islam, especially in the south where you're traveling, there's a deep layer of Berber (Amazigh) spirituality that predates Islam by millennia.

### The Layers
Islam arrived in Morocco in the 7th century but didn't replace Berber beliefs — it absorbed them. What you see today is a syncretic blend: orthodox Sunni Islam on top, Sufi mysticism in the middle, and pre-Islamic animism underneath. In cities like Marrakech the orthodox layer dominates. In the Anti-Atlas and coast (Taghazout, Sidi Ifni, Mirleft), the older layers surface.

### Marabout Shrines — The White Domes
The white-domed tombs you'll see dotting every hillside and beach are marabout shrines — tombs of local saints believed to carry بركة / barakah (blessing/spiritual power). This بركة doesn't die with the saint — it stays in the tomb, radiating to anyone who visits.

What people do there: light candles, pray for healing, tie cloth strips to nearby trees, hold مواسم / moussems (pilgrimage festivals). Families bring newborns to be placed under a saint's protection. At Sidi Kaouki (near Essaouira), families bring children for their first haircut at the marabout of a 19th-century healer who treated infertility.

Marabout Beach in Mirleft is literally named after the ancient tomb perched above the sand. Sidi Ifni has one at the northern end of the beach where the cemetery dominates the hillside.

This is controversial with orthodox Islam — saint veneration is technically شرك / shirk (associating others with God). But in rural Morocco it's just... how things are.

### The Argan Tree — Sacred
For Amazigh communities in the Souss-Massa region (exactly where you're going), the argan tree is sacred. According to folklore, God created it as a gift for humanity. Its gnarled branches are believed to hold mystical powers. Locals tie cloth strips to its base and leave offerings — for harvest, healing, protection.

Argan oil isn't just cosmetic — it's used in wedding rituals, childbirth ceremonies, and healing. The tree marks tribal and family boundaries. A motherly figure that feeds, shelters, and protects.

### Gnawa — Music as Spirit Medicine
The Gnawa are ritual musicians originally from Sub-Saharan Africa, brought to Morocco as slaves in the 15th-16th century. They heal through music that induces trance. UNESCO recognized it as Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2019.

The ليلة / lila (literally "night") is an all-night ceremony where Gnawa musicians play the قنبري / guembri (three-stringed bass lute) and قراقب / qraqeb (metal castanets) to summon specific spirits called ملوك / mluk ("the owners"). Each spirit has its own color, incense, and music. Participants enter trance — physical and emotional responses believed to be the spirits manifesting. The trance is therapeutic: healing mental distress, emotional suffering, even physical paralysis.

Essaouira is the capital of Gnawa culture. You'll recognize Gnawa musicians by their sky-blue djellabas, embroidered tunics, and caps decorated with cowrie shells. The annual Gnaoua Festival draws 500,000 people but runs in June — during your visit, look for Gnawa performers in the medina and at Salut Maroc rooftop (Fri-Sun).

### الجن / Jinn
Belief in الجن / jinn — supernatural spirits capable of good and evil — is woven into daily life. The most famous is عيشة قنديشة / Aicha Kandicha, a powerful female jinn who appears as a beautiful woman near water, then reveals her true form. شمهروش / Shamharoush is the king of الجن / jinn, head of the jinn court — people seek his favor to end possession.

This isn't folklore-for-tourists. People genuinely believe. Gnawa lila ceremonies are often held because someone is suspected to be possessed by a جن.

### What You'll Notice
- خمسة / Khamsa (Hand of Fatima) everywhere — jewelry, walls, keychains, embroidered on clothes. Protects against the evil eye. The خميسة / khmissa brooch is pinned to newborns' clothing
- Silver jewelry with coral — silver symbolizes life and protection in Berber tradition, coral wards off misfortune
- Henna patterns with meaning — not just decoration. Symbols convey blessings, ward off evil, express hopes for fertility and health. The five-pointed star on palms connects the wearer to spiritual protection. Knowledge passes mother to daughter
- White-domed marabout shrines on hilltops and beaches — active pilgrimage sites, not ruins
- Cloth strips tied to argan trees and sacred sites
- Call to prayer 5x daily from every minaret — the soundscape of the country

### Women's Spiritual Authority
In Berber culture, women hold significant spiritual power. Healing practices — herbalism, midwifery, henna rituals — pass through the matrilineal line. Elements of witchcraft and sorcery that don't involve Arabic writing (which is men's domain via Quranic verses) are the preserve of women. In the Souss Valley (exactly where your trip goes), beliefs about women's magical authority are especially strong.

### Sacred Geography on Your Route
- Marrakech: Seven Saints pilgrimage, sacred geometry in Bahia Palace
- Essaouira: Gnawa capital, Sidi Kaouki marabout 25km south (healing saint for fertility)
- Paradise Valley: Sacred natural springs in an arid landscape, a spiritual sanctuary. The emerald pools aren't just pretty, they're holy
- Sidi Ifni: Marabout tomb at the northern beach, cemetery on the hillside
- Mirleft: Marabout Beach named after the ancient tomb above the sand
- Legzira: The natural arch — locals describe sunsets under it as spiritual, a gateway between worlds

### How It Differs from Egyptian Islam
- Saint veneration is way more prominent — every village has at least one marabout
- Community law often trumps Quranic law in rural Berber areas
- Nomadic Berbers are exempt from Ramadan fasting
- Spirit possession ceremonies (lila) are mainstream, not fringe
- Women hold real spiritual authority through healing and ritual
- The Islam here absorbed the land's older beliefs instead of replacing them — the white domes, sacred trees, jinn stories all coexist with Friday prayer

## Survival Tips
### Ramadan (Feb 17 – Mar 19)
- Most local restaurants close daytime or limited hours (10am-4pm)
- Tourist hotels, riads, international chains stay open
- Don't eat/drink/smoke publicly during daylight — respect, not law
- Eat in your accommodation during the day
- Alcohol harder to find — high-end hotels serve indoors with food
- ⚠️ Avoid traveling 6-7:30pm — be at destination by 5:30pm (إفطار shutdown)
- Streets quieter during day, VERY lively after sunset
- Dinner service starts later (8pm+) to align with إفطار

### Scam Awareness
- "Helpful" strangers = fake guides. Say "لا شكراً" and keep walking
- If lost, go INTO a shop and ask owner (won't leave their store)
- Photos at Jemaa el-Fnaa = they WILL demand 20-50 MAD
- Taxi change scam — have exact cash
- Someone says "it's closed" = ignore, verify yourself

### Money
- ATMs everywhere (Visa/Mastercard), dispense 100/200 MAD — break them immediately
- Keep 500-1000 MAD in small bills (20s, 50s) daily
- Cash > cards for bargaining power
- 1 EUR ≈ 11 MAD

### Bargaining
- Seller quotes price → quarter it (they'll laugh = game starts)
- Final price = 1/3 to 1/2 of initial quote
- Shop early morning = first sale is "good luck", better deals
- Use Arabic! "غالي كتير" (too expensive), "أنا مصري" (I'm Egyptian)
- Be polite, friendly — it's a sport, not a battle

### Food & Safety
- Busy street food stalls = safe (high turnover), empty stalls = avoid
- Bottled water only, no tap water
- Covered shoulders/knees in religious areas
- Medina at night = safe in main areas, avoid dark alleys alone
- Sunset ~6:30pm — plan rooftop drinks for 6pm

### Shopping
Real argan oil = pharmacies (80-120 MAD), NOT souks (often fake).

### Medical
Pharmacies everywhere, sell antibiotics OTC. If severe diarrhea: Imodium + ORS packets first, buy Azithromycin at pharmacy if needed.

### Quick Ref
- 1 EUR ≈ 11 MAD
- Street food: 20-40 MAD (€2-4)
- Restaurant: 80-150 MAD (€7-14)
- Rooftop cocktail: 120-170 MAD (€11-15)
